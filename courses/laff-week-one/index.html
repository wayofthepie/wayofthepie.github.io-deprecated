Dot Product

Let $x, y \in \reals^n$ $$ x = \begin{pmatrix} \chi_0 \\ \chi_1 \\ \vdots \\ \chi_{n-1} \end{pmatrix} \text{ and } y = \begin{pmatrix} \psi_0 \\ \psi_1 \\ \vdots \\ \psi_{n-1} \end{pmatrix} $$

Then the dot product (inner product) of $x$ and $y$ is given by

$$ dot(x,y) = \chi_0 \psi_0 + \chi_1 \psi_1 + \cdots + \chi_{n-1} \psi_{n-1} = \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} \chi_i \psi_i $$

So if:

$$ x = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 4 \\ \end{pmatrix} \text{ and } y = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \\ -1 \\ \end{pmatrix} \\ $$

It would give:

$$ dot(x,y) = dot \bigg(\begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 4 \\ \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \\ -1 \\ \end{pmatrix}\bigg) = (2 \cdot 1) + (-1 \cdot -2) + (4 \cdot -1) = 0 $$

Alternative Notation

The dot product can also be defined as follows.

$$ dot(x,y) = x^Ty $$

If $x$ is a column vector $x^T$ is a row vector. So we get the following:

$$ \begin{aligned} &\begin{pmatrix} \chi_0 \\ \chi_1 \\ \vdots \\ \chi_{n-1} \end{pmatrix}^T \begin{pmatrix} \psi_0 \\ \psi_1 \\ \vdots \\ \psi_{n-1} \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} \chi_0 \enspace \chi_1 \enspace \dots \enspace \chi_{n-1} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \psi_0 \\ \psi_1 \\ \vdots \\ \psi_{n-1} \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \chi_0 \psi_0 + \chi_1 \psi_1 + \dots + \chi_{n-1} \psi_{n-1} \end{aligned} $$

Vector Length

Size of a vector is num components. Length is euclidian length. Given a vector $x$ such that

$$ x = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ -3 \\ \end{pmatrix} $$

The length of $x$ is $\sqrt{4^2+3^2} = 5$. Pythagoras' theorem! We can also get the length of a vector $x$ using $\sqrt{dot(x,x)}$.

Let $x \in \reals^2$ then the length is

$$ \begin{aligned} \sqrt{dot(x,x)} &= \sqrt{x^Tx} \\ &= \sqrt{ \begin{pmatrix} \chi_0 \\ \chi_1 \\ \vdots \\ \chi_{n-1} \end{pmatrix}^T \begin{pmatrix} \chi_0 \\ \chi_1 \\ \vdots \\ \chi_{n-1} \end{pmatrix} }\\ &= \sqrt{\chi_{0}^{2} + \chi_{1}^{2} + \cdots + \chi_{n-1}^2} = \sqrt{ \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} \chi_{i}^{2} } \end{aligned} $$

In summary,

  • The euclidian length of a vector equals the square root of the sum of the squares of its components.
  • The length also equals the square root of the dot product of the vector with itself.

Unit vs Unit Basis Vector

Unit vector is a vector of length $1$. Unit basis (standard basis vectors) have all zeros except for a single entry which is one. They are denoted in this course with $e_j$ where $j$ is the index of the $1$ in the vector.
