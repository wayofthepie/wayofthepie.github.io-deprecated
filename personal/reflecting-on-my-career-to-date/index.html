I started my software career in April 2011, in EMC, as an intern in the Build and Release Engineering team. Almost 10 years ago. For the first four years, I worked mostly on Operations, our internal build systems, and our production systems. There was a sprinkle of development scattered throughout those years. In 2015 our group started the journey towards a DevOps transformation.

It took us about a year and a half before all our devs were comfortable enough to continuously deploy to production. But it was an amazing transformation. We went from releases every 2 months in a giant, insanely architected system (more on that later), with separate Ops/QA/Dev teams, to 60+ releases a day with the team writing the software owning it to production and beyond. The new system was 100+ networked services, most folks did TDD, overall the quality of what we delivered and how delivered it was orders of magnitude better.

I'll talk more about that group and my time there soon. I left EMC/Dell in June 2018 and went to McKesson. My role there was similar but applied globally. The goal was to improve how developers created and delivered software, globally. Looking back, we did quite a lot to guide the company. I feel like I accomplished a lot in the 2 years, 7 months I was there. More on that later too.

I left McKesson in December 2020 and since then I've spent some time thinking about what my next career move will be. Before I do that, I want to reflect on the last 10 years in more depth. But even before that, why did I choose this career in the first place?

What have I accomplished, both personally and with others? What insane things have I seen? Maybe then I might have better insight as to what I want to do next.

From Photos To Films

That lasted a few months. After that, I didn't do much with computers until I was in my mid-teens. I still played games, but it was mostly on the PSX, and PS2 when that came out. When I was in fourth year, I think I was 15 at the time, our class entered a film competition. If I remember correctly, it was about the dangers of drinking. I was a bit wild back then and used to drink a lot anyway so I found it hilarious! But, I enjoyed that project. I was the editor, and it brought back the joy I had when using Photoshop a few years earlier.

I don't remember what editing software I was using, maybe Sony Vega. But I spent hours on that film. There is one scene I remember the most. It was the climax of the movie. One of the girls gets drunk and injures herself. The scene was at a party and was filmed as a timelapse. I played around a bit with how best to show the impact alcohol was having on her. The final version had five or six short time jumps from the start of the party when she gets injured. With each jump to a new scene, the film slowed down a few fractions of a second. After two jumps it made the viewer feel uneasy, after three it was quite clear something bad was going on, the music became more ominous. This along with the great acting in that scene - I am imagining Oscar-worthy acting here - making it the best part of the film by far. In the climax of the overall scene, the girl falls over and breaks her arm or something - not sure exactly what - and it looked awesome in super slow motion.

After this, I didn't do much with computers again, until I thought I wanted to be a game developer and started a course in game design when I was 18.

Linux and Hacking

Before I dropped out - spoiler alert I did drop out in the end - I was introduced to a bunch of things that set me on my way to where I am now. The first was Linux. I lived with one of the guys on our course and he introduced me to Backtrack Linux. This was the coolest thing I had ever seen. Within a few weeks I was cracking our neighbours WEP based routers, and getting free internet. WEP was a poorly implemented security protocol in use on most wireless routers in the 2000's/2010's. It's been a while, but I think the attack involved replaying captured ARP packets, which would cause the target router to have to generate a new IV (Initialization Vector), and then we would capture that and replay, continue until you have enough IV's and data to crack the WEP key.

This was not a good thing to do. It was a terrible thing to do. But I did it anyway, and I enjoyed it. I was a dumb kid. Regardless of the ethics, it's what got me seriously interested in computers.

The other thing I was introduced to was Java. This was the coolest thing ever to me at the time. We did Visual Basic, ActionScript (Flash ðŸ˜¬), and Java in the course. So of course Java was amazing!

Onto Computer Science

I decided, just after the first semester of the Game Design course, that I would prefer to do Computer Science. So, I dropped out of the course overall. I did still repeat maths that year and ended up doing well. Because of that, I could apply for college. I did, and got in and became a CS student! From day one I loved it. Since my friend had introduced me to Linux I had been doing a lot more tinkering with it. I had pretty much all but forgotten about all the security tools on backtrack (I was still using this at the time) and was more interested in how the hell this thing ever turned on.

We didn't cover anything to do with Operating Systems in the first year, however. We mostly did PHP. Which probably sounds crazy to you right now.
